ADHD A–Z Glossary

Explore clear, easy-to-understand definitions of ADHD-related terms.

ABCDE FGHIJ KLMNO PQRST UVWXYZ

A

ADHD: Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder — a neurodevelopmental condition affecting attention, impulsivity, and energy regulation.

Attention: The ability to focus on a task without being easily distracted.

B

Burnout: Emotional, physical, and mental exhaustion caused by prolonged stress or masking.

C

Comorbidity: When ADHD occurs alongside another condition such as anxiety, depression, or autism.

D

Dopamine: A neurotransmitter linked to motivation and reward, often imbalanced in ADHD.

E

Executive Function: Mental processes that help with planning, prioritizing, and completing tasks — often affected in ADHD.

F

Focus: The ability to maintain attention on a single task, which may fluctuate in ADHD.

G

Goal Paralysis: Feeling stuck or unable to begin a task because it feels too overwhelming.

H

Hyperfocus: Intense concentration on a specific task or interest, sometimes for hours at a time.

I

Impulsivity: Acting quickly without considering consequences, often driven by strong emotions or excitement.

J

Journaling: A reflective writing practice that can support emotional regulation and self-awareness.

K

Kickstart Strategy: A method to help initiate tasks, such as using timers or body doubling.

L

Low Frustration Tolerance: Becoming easily overwhelmed when tasks feel difficult or delayed.

M

Masking: Concealing ADHD traits to fit social expectations, often leading to exhaustion.

N

Neurodivergent: A term for people whose brain wiring differs from the neurotypical norm.

O

Overstimulation: Feeling overwhelmed by excessive sensory input like noise, light, or activity.

P

Procrastination: Delaying tasks due to executive dysfunction, perfectionism, or task paralysis.

Q

Quiet Rest: Taking intentional downtime to reset sensory and emotional overload.

R

Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD): Intense emotional response to perceived criticism or rejection.

S

Stimming: Repetitive movements or actions that help self-regulate or focus.

T

Time Blindness: Difficulty sensing or managing the passage of time accurately.

U

Understimulation: Lack of mental engagement causing boredom or restlessness.

V

Visual Cues: Using color or visuals to aid memory and structure tasks.

W

Working Memory: The ability to hold information briefly for use — often weaker in ADHD.

X

X-Factor: A unique creativity or intuition often seen in ADHD minds.

Y

Yielding: Learning to rest and pace energy instead of pushing through fatigue.

Z

Zone of Focus: The ideal balance between distraction and hyperfocus where attention flows naturally.
